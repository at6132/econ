# 03 — Primitives Spec

> **This is the most important document in the spec.** Every business players invent must be expressible as a composition of the primitives below. If something they want to do *cannot* be expressed, the primitives are wrong — fix them, don't add a special case.

---

## What "primitive" means here

A primitive is an **economic atom**: an entity, resource, or capability that the engine knows about natively. Players compose primitives into businesses. The engine doesn't know what a "shipping company" is — it knows about plots, vessels (composed of matter + capital), labor, and contracts. A shipping company is what emerges when a player composes those.

There are **9 primitives** in v1. Each one has: a definition, properties, what players can do with it, and worked examples.

---

## Primitive 1 — Land / Plots

**Definition:** A bounded region of the world with physical properties. Players claim plots; plots are property; plots are tradeable.

**Properties (intrinsic, set at world generation):**
- Coordinates (x, y on the world grid)
- Area (some plots are bigger than others)
- Terrain type (plains, forest, mountain, desert, tundra, swamp, water-shallow, water-deep)
- Climate (temperature, rainfall, seasonality)
- Coastal access (yes/no, harbor depth if yes)
- Altitude
- Hidden subsurface composition (what's actually under the ground — *not visible until prospected*)

**Properties (derived, change over time):**
- Owner (player ID or null)
- Improvements built on it (buildings, infrastructure, code-services hosted here)
- Known surveys (what's been discovered about the subsurface)
- Current usage (what's being produced/extracted/operated)

**Player capabilities:**
- Claim an unclaimed plot (subject to availability rules per game mode)
- Sell/transfer a plot to another player
- Lease a plot to another player (for a duration, at a price)
- Build on a plot
- Survey the plot (costs time and money, reveals subsurface info)
- Subdivide (maybe, future feature)

**Note on subsurface:** Resources do not "exist" as designer-declared facts. World generation produces a hidden composition map. A plot might have heavy iron content, light copper content, and a small oil pocket — but until someone surveys it, no one (not even the owner) knows that. This makes prospecting a real activity and creates information markets.

## Primitive 2 — Matter / Materials

**Definition:** Physical stuff. Has properties. Conserved. Cannot be created from nothing.

**Properties:**
- Material ID (auto-generated by world; players can name it)
- Physical properties (mass, density, melting point, conductivity, caloric value, durability, etc. — a fixed schema of attributes the engine tracks)
- Quantity (units exist, are owned, can be moved)
- Location (always somewhere — plot, vehicle, warehouse)

**Critical design choice — abstraction level:**
We have two reasonable options, and this is one of the biggest open design questions:

- **Option A: Realistic materials.** The world has familiar materials (iron, copper, wheat, oil, etc.) with realistic properties. Players name them, but the underlying chemistry is real.
- **Option B: Generic materials with properties.** The world has materials with attributes; players discover what combinations are useful for what. "Material #47 has density X, conductivity Y" — players figure out it's a good wire.

Option A is faster to ship and more intuitive. Option B is more emergent and more interesting. **For v1, go with A** — pre-defined real materials with realistic properties. Revisit B as a future expansion.

**Player capabilities:**
- Extract from plot (if subsurface contains the material and you have extraction infrastructure)
- Transform via production (combine materials + labor + energy → new materials, see Primitive 6)
- Move (between locations, takes time per Primitive 4)
- Trade (sell to another player, see Primitive 7)
- Store (warehouses on plots)
- Destroy (rare, but possible — fire, decay)

## Primitive 3 — Labor / Agents

**Definition:** Time-bounded capacity to do work. Comes from players themselves, hired NPCs, or AI agents in solo mode.

**Properties:**
- Skill levels (e.g. extraction efficiency, production quality, negotiation, surveying)
- Loyalty / morale (affects reliability)
- Wage (what they cost per unit time)
- Location (labor must physically be where the work is)

**Three sources of labor:**
1. **Player time.** The player themselves. Limited by real-world hours played. Most valuable.
2. **Hired NPC labor.** Generic workers, recruitable in populated areas, cost wages, have skills, can quit if mistreated.
3. **AI agent labor (solo mode only).** AI agents can be employed by other AI agents or by the human player.

**Note:** Labor is *not* infinite. Populated regions have larger labor pools. Frontier plots have to import labor at cost. This makes geography matter for labor too.

**Player capabilities:**
- Hire labor (post a job, set wages, applicants accept/reject)
- Assign labor to tasks
- Train labor (raise skill, costs time and money)
- Fire labor
- Be hired by others (yes — players can earn wages from other players)

---

## Primitive 4 — Time, Distance, and Movement

**Definition:** Things take time. Goods take time to move. Distance is real.

**Properties:**
- World tick (the smallest time unit; see "Time scale" in doc 09)
- Movement speed (depends on transport mode and goods)
- Travel routes (paths between locations, may have toll/permission requirements)

**Player capabilities:**
- Move goods, labor, money between locations
- Build/own transport assets (vessels, vehicles)
- Sell transport services to other players (this is the "shipping company" use case)
- Plan routes, optimize for cost vs speed

**Note:** Time is the universal currency. Energy and labor convert into time-saved or time-required. Most of the strategic depth in Realm comes from time being a real cost.

---

## Primitive 5 — Capital / Money

**Definition:** A medium of exchange. Tracked on accounts. Can be loaned, invested, lost.

**v1 design:** Single in-game currency, issued by the simulation as a starting grant per player and via NPC seed transactions. Currency cannot be printed by anyone except via designed mechanisms.

**v2+ design:** Player-issued currencies. A player can declare "I issue Avi-Coin, redeemable for X." Other players choose to accept or not. A foreign exchange market emerges. This is HARD and NOT v1.

**Properties of accounts:**
- Owner (player ID or business ID)
- Balance
- Transaction history (visible to owner; partially visible to public for businesses)

**Player capabilities:**
- Send money to another account
- Receive money
- Take loans (see Primitive 8 — Contracts)
- Issue stocks/bonds (later, via Contracts and the financial layer)
- Hold capital in different forms (cash, on-deposit, invested in inventory, invested in plots)

**Note:** The single most important rule is **money cannot be created or destroyed except through designed channels** (initial grants, scheduled NPC injections in solo mode, etc.). Any path to printing money breaks the economy. This must be enforced at the data layer, not at the application layer.

---

## Primitive 6 — Production

**Definition:** A process that consumes inputs (matter, labor, energy, money) at a location over time and produces outputs (matter, services).

**Properties:**
- Recipe (what inputs → what outputs at what rate). **Players define their own recipes** by configuring buildings and equipment, within physical-law constraints. The engine validates conservation and energy balance.
- Throughput (rate at which the process runs)
- Efficiency (affected by skill, equipment quality, etc.)

**v1 simplification:** We provide a library of pre-built "recipe templates" for common production. Players unlock these by building the right equipment. **The recipe library is generative, not curated:** anything that satisfies physical-law constraints (mass balance, energy balance) is a valid recipe. We just hand-author the most obvious starting templates.

**Player capabilities:**
- Operate production on a plot
- Configure recipes within physical constraints
- Scale up (more equipment = more throughput)
- Optimize (better skilled labor = higher efficiency)

## Primitive 7 — Markets and Trade

**Definition:** Mechanisms by which players exchange goods, services, and capital. Two layers exist:

### 7a. Direct P2P exchange
Two players agree on a transfer: "I give you 100 iron for $500." Atomic, mutual, one-shot.

### 7b. Order books / exchanges
Where there's enough volume in a good or asset, an open order book emerges. Buy/sell orders are public. Anyone can place orders. The exchange matches them.

**v1 design:** Both 7a and 7b exist. Order books exist for any commodity that hits a volume threshold (e.g., 5 distinct sellers in the past 30 game-days). Below threshold, players use direct P2P trades.

**Properties of an order book:**
- Asset (what's being traded — a material, a service, a share)
- Bids and asks (price, quantity, location of delivery)
- Last trade price
- Volume / depth

**Player capabilities:**
- Post bids and asks (limit orders)
- Take an existing order (market order)
- See public order book (with delay if information cost is enabled)
- Sell privately via P2P (off-book, can negotiate non-standard terms)
- Build market-making businesses (provide liquidity, earn spread)

## Primitive 8 — Contracts

**Definition:** Multi-step, multi-condition agreements between parties, enforced by the engine.

This is the **most important non-obvious primitive.** Real economies don't run on instant trades — they run on long-term contracts. Without first-class contracts, players reinvent them poorly using promises and reputation.

**A contract has:**
- Parties (who's involved — players, businesses, the engine)
- Triggers (when does each clause activate — time, event, threshold)
- Obligations (what must be done — pay, deliver, perform service)
- Penalties (what happens on breach)
- Duration / termination conditions

**Worked examples:**
- *Supply contract:* "Party A delivers 100 iron to Party B's plot every 7 game-days for 12 weeks. Party B pays $X per delivery. Penalty for non-delivery: $Y. Penalty for non-payment: lose access to next delivery."
- *Loan contract:* "Party A receives $1000 from Party B. Party A pays $50 in interest every 30 game-days for 12 months, then returns principal. Penalty for default: forfeit collateral plot to Party B."
- *Employment contract:* "Worker performs labor at Party's plot for $W per game-day for 90 days. Either side may terminate with 7 days' notice."
- *Equity contract:* "Investor pays $X for Y% ownership of Party's business. Investor receives Y% of declared profits."
- *Service subscription:* "Customer pays $S per month. Provider's code-service runs on customer's account."

**Key design rule:** Contracts are *templates plus parameters,* not free-form code. The engine ships a library of contract templates (trade, loan, employment, equity, service). Players parameterize them. Building new templates is a designer-only activity initially; later we expose template-creation to advanced players.

**Player capabilities:**
- Propose a contract
- Accept / reject / counter-propose
- Monitor active contracts
- Breach (with penalty) or honor
- View public history of a counterparty's contracts (reputation surface)

## Primitive 9 — Code / Programmable Services

**Definition:** Player-written logic that runs on the engine, can be invoked by other players or by other code, and can be sold as a service.

This is the **moat primitive.** No other game in this genre has it. It's also the hardest to build.

**v1 design:**
- A safe sandboxed scripting environment (probably Lua, possibly a visual block-based language for low-skill players with a Lua escape hatch — see doc 07).
- Code can read public game state (prices, plots, public contract terms).
- Code can read private state owned by the player who deployed it (their inventory, accounts) — but **not** other players' private state, ever.
- Code can take actions (place orders, propose contracts, send messages) on behalf of the deploying player.
- Code consumes computational resources (CPU/memory budget per tick) — the player pays for this in in-game currency.

**Worked examples (services that emerge):**
- *Auto-restock:* "When my inventory of iron drops below X, automatically place a buy order at market price."
- *Trading bot:* "Watch this commodity. Buy when 7-day MA crosses 30-day MA. Sell on opposite cross."
- *Logistics optimizer:* "Given a list of deliveries, compute the cheapest route." Sold as a SaaS to shipping companies.
- *Market data aggregator:* "Public dashboard of all commodity prices across all exchanges. Subscription fee for premium analytics."
- *Credit scorer:* "Given a counterparty ID, return a credit score based on their public contract history." Lenders pay per query.
- *Game inside the game:* A player-built betting market on event outcomes.

**Player capabilities:**
- Write code in the in-game IDE
- Deploy code (becomes a "service" running on the deployer's account)
- Set pricing (subscription, per-call, free)
- Subscribe to / call other players' services
- See usage metrics for your own services
- Update / version / retire your own services

**Constraints:**
- Code must be deterministic given inputs (no true randomness — random comes from a tick-stamped seed for reproducibility)
- Code has a CPU budget per tick; over budget = code throttled or charged premium
- Code cannot create money, materials, or labor from nothing (Primitive 2 / Pillar 2)
- Code cannot read other players' private state
- Code cannot impersonate other players

**Doc 07 covers this primitive in much greater detail.** It's the largest single chunk of v2 design work.

## Composing primitives — worked examples

### A shipping company
- 1+ coastal **plots** (warehouses, dock)
- **Capital** to buy vessels (vessels are **matter** with high transport speed)
- Hired **labor** (captains, dockworkers)
- **Movement** capability (the core service — moving other players' goods)
- **Contracts** (supply contracts with shippers, employment contracts with crew)
- Revenue from per-shipment pricing or subscription contracts
- Optionally: **code** that handles route optimization, automated dispatching

### A SaaS company (no physical footprint)
- 1 plot (any plot, even cheap inland — needed for hosting equipment)
- Modest **capital**
- **Code** that provides the service
- **Contracts** (subscription contracts with users)
- Possibly **labor** (engineers to maintain code, customer-support contracts)
- Revenue from service subscriptions

### A bank
- 1+ plots (vault buildings)
- Substantial **capital** (initial reserves)
- **Reputation** (built over time)
- **Contracts** (loan contracts with borrowers, deposit contracts with depositors)
- Possibly **code** (credit-scoring, automated underwriting)
- Revenue from spread between deposit interest and loan interest

### A speculator / trader
- 1 small plot (just to exist somewhere)
- **Capital**
- Heavy use of **markets** (order books)
- Possibly **code** (trading bots, analytics)
- Possibly subscriptions to others' **code** (market-data services)
- Revenue from buying low and selling high

### A research / surveying firm
- Multiple plots **leased**, not owned (visit, survey, leave)
- Hired **labor** (skilled surveyors)
- **Capital** for equipment
- **Contracts** (paid by plot owners to survey their land, or speculatively surveying unclaimed land)
- Revenue from per-survey fees or sale of survey data

If all five of these compose cleanly from the 9 primitives, the primitives are working. **If you find a business idea that doesn't compose, the primitive set is incomplete. Add what's missing before continuing.**

## Primitives we considered and rejected (for v1)

- **Politics / governance.** Yes, eventually. Not in v1. Too easy for it to dominate the design before the economy is mature.
- **Combat / military.** No. Out of scope. Maybe forever.
- **Player-issued currencies.** Yes, eventually. Too risky for v1 — single currency keeps the simulation tractable.
- **Family / inheritance.** No. Players are players, not characters with mortality.
- **NPCs as deep characters with personalities (beyond the named AI rivals in solo mode).** No. We're not building Stardew Valley.

These are conscious cuts, not oversights. Adding them later is fine. Adding them too early is what kills projects like this.
